What this is

DiffSinger can generate expressive sung audio from musical scores, but setting up the full training workflow from scratch is a project in itself. This repo wraps the OpenVPI ecosystem — DiffSinger, SOME, Montreal Forced Aligner — into one reproducible workflow you can actually follow start to finish.

It is designed for musicians and ML hobbyists who want to build something real, not just run someone else's demo.

The pipeline

1

Record

Sing short phrases into a dry mono signal at 44.1 kHz. Clean recordings are the foundation — get close to the mic, keep the room quiet, and aim for consistency across sessions.

2

Label

Scaffold the CSV, type in your lyrics. This is where each clip gets its identity: the text, the expected phonemes, the eventual note data.

3

Align

Montreal Forced Aligner takes your audio and your lyrics and produces timed phoneme sequences — it figures out exactly when each sound happens.

4

Extract

SOME listens to the pitch contour of each clip and converts it into discrete note names and durations. Melody becomes notation.

5

Train

Binarize your dataset, then hand it off to DiffSinger. A GPU makes this a matter of hours rather than days. The model learns to map notation back to your voice.

6

Render

Feed the trained model a score, get sung audio back. That's your voice — synthesized from scratch.

The CSV contract

Everything flows through a single CSV file: one row per clip, eight columns, no ambiguity. Each stage of the pipeline reads from it and writes back to it. When something breaks, the CSV is where you look first — source of truth, audit trail, and handoff point between every tool in the chain.

Lessons learned the hard way

Back up the CSV before any bulk operation. Forced alignment will happily overwrite 200 rows at once, and a timestamped backup from thirty seconds ago is a much better friend than git blame.
Keep commas out of your lyrics. The CSV is comma-delimited, and quoting fields to work around it creates subtle parsing bugs that surface at the worst possible moment.
Fifty to a hundred clips is enough to start. You don't need a full album's worth of recordings. Start smaller, iterate faster, add more data once the pipeline is proven end-to-end.
Mark bad clips — don't delete them. Use the exclude: flag. The WAV stays on disk, the row stays in the CSV, and you keep the option to revisit. Deleted data has a way of looking useful a week later.
GPU time is not optional in practice. A T4 or L4 will have a model trained in hours. CPU training is technically possible and will keep you company for several days.

Ethics

This toolkit is designed for training on your own voice — consent is not an afterthought, it is the premise.
